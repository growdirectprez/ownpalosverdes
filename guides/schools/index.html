PVPUSD School Guide

Palos Verdes Peninsula Unified School District serves roughly 10,300 students across 17 schools in four cities. Assignment rules vary by level. This guide covers how it actually works — from a parent who navigated the system with two kids.

Overview

How Assignment Works

PVPUSD uses different assignment rules at each level. This is the single most important thing relocating families need to understand before they start looking at houses.

Elementary (TK–5)

Your address determines your assigned elementary school — it's boundary-based. Enter it into the PVPUSD School Locator and it tells you where your child will go. But assignment is only finalized after enrollment is complete — if your home school is "impacted," the district can place your child at the closest school with available space. This happens at popular schools like Montemalaga, Lunada Bay, and Silver Spur in strong enrollment years.

Intermediate (6–8)

Middle school (PVPUSD calls them "intermediate") is boundary-assigned across three campuses: PVIS on the PVE side, Miraleste on the eastern RPV side, and Ridgecrest covering RHE, Rolling Hills, and western RPV. The intradistrict transfer window — February 16–28, notifications March 3 — is one of the most important calendar items for Hill parents.

High School (9–12)

Any student residing in PVPUSD may attend either Palos Verdes High or Peninsula High. High school is not boundary-assigned — it's open choice. Where you buy a house does not determine which high school your child attends. The two schools have meaningfully different schedules and cultures — this is a real choice families make.

The "Impacted" Caveat

Assignment is only finalized after enrollment is complete. If your home school is impacted at that point, the district places your child at "the closest school with available space." This is not theoretical. It happens at popular boundary schools in hot enrollment years. I tell every relocating family: check with the district office before you close escrow, not after.

Dual Immersion

Cornerstone Elementary

Cornerstone is PVPUSD's Spanish dual-immersion program. It has no attendance boundary — any district-eligible student can apply. The majority of the school day and all core content are taught in Spanish; PE is in English.

Application window: March 1 – March 31, through the Aeries parent portal.

Notifications: End of April.

Priority: Lunada Bay residents go first (Cornerstone is the Lunada Bay campus host site). All other district students enter a lottery if seats remain.

No waitlist for denied applications. If you don't get in, there is no queue for future openings.

Parents who want Cornerstone should apply before deciding on an intra-district transfer for their boundary school. The timelines run independently.

The Transfer Window Matters

Parents who work at opposite ends of the peninsula, carpool across the hill, or have kids at different-side schools regularly use the intradistrict transfer window (February 16–28, notifications March 3) to move a child to a more logistically practical campus. I see this constantly: a family buys on the RHE side but has work or family ties on the PVE side and wants PVIS. Approvals are space-dependent, not guaranteed, and accepting a transfer means giving up your boundary placement. Build that into your home search, not after.

Grades 9–12

High Schools — The Decision

This is the choice families actually make. Both PV High and Peninsula High are top-performing, high-ranking California high schools. The difference is not quality — it's fit. The schedule structure is the most decision-relevant fact, and families relocating from out of state often assume high school is boundary-assigned like elementary. It is not. Any PVPUSD resident can enroll at either campus.

PVPUSD also operates Rancho Del Mar High (38 Crest Road West, Rolling Hills), a WASC-accredited continuation school.

Out-of-District

Getting In From Outside

This is the section that matters most for buyers who own or are considering a home outside PVPUSD boundaries but want access to the schools. These paths are real, documented, and used regularly by peninsula families.

Grandparent Permit

The most commonly used out-of-district path. Available TK through 12. The student must have at least one grandparent residing inside PVPUSD boundaries. LAUSD-zoned portions of RHE and RPV (notably parts of Eastview) do not qualify as "inside PVPUSD boundaries."

Application window: April 1 – June 1 (annual). Space-dependent — approval is not guaranteed. The district recommends concurrent enrollment in your home district as a fallback.

Documents required:

  • Student's official birth certificate
  • Parent's official birth certificate (proves the parent–grandparent lineage) — this is the piece most applicants forget
  • Two different utility bills showing the grandparent's PVPUSD-boundary address
  • Recent report card or transcript
  • IEP or 504 plan if applicable
  • An interdistrict permit release from the student's home district

Online enrollment note: when filling the district portal, enter the home address as literally "1 Grandparent Permit" — not the student's actual home address and not the grandparent's address.

The grandparent permit is a multi-generational legacy lever. It's how a family that moved "down the hill" years ago can still get grandchildren into PVPUSD today. Each child must file separately — permits are not portable across siblings.

Allen Bill (Parent Employment)

A parent works at least 10 hours per week at a business physically located inside PVPUSD boundaries. Governed by Administrative Regulation 5.111.12. Covers K–12. Space-dependent. This path is underused — a buyer who works out of Peninsula Center, Golden Cove, or any PVE/RPV/RHE business has a viable path they may not know about.

Eastview Optional Attendance

This is not a permit — it's a zoning fact. Eastview (eastern RPV, adjoining San Pedro) is technically zoned for LAUSD. PVPUSD designates it an optional attendance area — TK–12 students who live there may choose PVPUSD instead. No lineage, employment, or military tie required.

Active-Duty Military

Children of active-duty U.S. military parents qualify for an interdistrict permit through a separate, expedited process.

The Common Failure Point

Interdistrict releases from the home district are where most applications stall. LAUSD, for example, has its own release process that takes time and doesn't always approve. If you're counting on a grandparent permit or Allen Bill path, start the home-district release early. Build it into your timeline, not as an afterthought.

Independent

Private Schools

The peninsula is unusual in that its public schools are as strong as its privates for most families. The reasons families go private tend to be specific: class size, K–12 continuity, values-based education, or learning-difference support — not performance.

Chadwick School

K–12

26800 Academy Drive, Rolling Hills side. Roughly 860 students, 8:1 student-teacher ratio. Tuition approximately $43,740–$51,240 depending on grade. Founded 1935. Outdoor education K–12, global studies and community service 7–12. The default name families mean when they say "Hill private school."

Rolling Hills Preparatory

6–12

Located in San Pedro (1 Rolling Hills Prep Way), not on the Hill, but serves peninsula families heavily. Tuition approximately $44,800. Academically rigorous with a strong learning-support program. 11 interscholastic sports.

Peninsula Heritage School

K–8

Rolling Hills Estates. Founded 1961. Small classes, around 12–15 students each. Emphasis on individualized education and a warm, small-community environment.

Rolling Hills Country Day

K–8

Palos Verdes Peninsula. K–5 elementary plus 6–8 middle school divisions. Small classes with subject-specialized teachers.

Faith-Based

Parochial Schools

A meaningful share of peninsula families — including families who could afford Chadwick — choose a religious K–8 for values, smaller scale, or proximity. The peninsula is well-served by Catholic and Lutheran options.

Three Lutheran K–8 schools sit on or near the peninsula boundary. Hill-native families know about them; relocating buyers typically don't.
